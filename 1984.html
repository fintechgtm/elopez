1984 Review: I love big brother

“Everything can be taken from a man but one thing: the last of the human freedoms—to choose one’s attitude in any given set of circumstances, to choose one’s own way.”― Viktor E. Frankl, Man's Search for Meaning

The end of George Orwell's 1984 is a sad and thought-provoking ending to the book's message about totalitarianism, rebellion, and the illusion of reality. I was rooting for Winston up until the end, hoping he could hold on to even an inch of his hate for big brother.

Similarities with Cuba

As a Cuban-American, I found myself often comparing Winston’s experience to my family’s. Before the internet came to Cuba, the island was truly closed off. Unless you worked in the heart of the capital, you wouldn’t come into contact with tourists. They weren’t even allowed to stay in the residential parts of the country. There was no cell phone access and very few people, if at all, could afford a telephone or make an international call.

The book is right in saying that when you’re closed off from the outside world and unable to compare your living situation with others, it’s hard to feel oppressed.

Doesn’t everyone have it this bad? Maybe worse?

Another similarity between Cuba and 1984 was the animosity they encouraged between citizens and the brainwashing. You were encouraged to watch your neighbors and family and inform the government whenever you witnessed someone breaking the rules. My uncle, who has long since passed away, had a hard time in Cuba because he was a gay man. A neighbor informed the party that he was homosexual and he was rounded up and incarcerated in a labor camp to “turn him” heterosexual.

It’s wild to think about how all of this closely resembles the thought police and joycamps.

Insights from the book

  1. Those who have a why to live, can bear with almost any how.
  2. True power is not in strength or money, but in the ability to control ideas.
  3. The human desire for freedom is inherent and unbreakable.
  4. Language has the power to shape and manipulate reality.
  5. If everyone believes something is true, does that make it true?
  6. Who controls the past controls the future. Who controls the present controls the past.
  7. For the great bulk of mankind, happiness is better than freedom.
  8. Knowledge is power, but it can also be a dangerous thing. Both to know it and be controlled by it. In a society where information is controlled, education can be a means of control.

So many unanswered questions

While the ending closed the loop on a lot of open threads, I left with so many unanswered questions. Here are some of my top questions:

  1. Did O’Brien actually co-author “The Book” or was that just another example of the Party taking credit for others’ work?
  2. Did they bait him with that photograph of the three prisoners or was its inclusion a mistake?
  3. If Winston purchased the journal from someone in the thought police, they knew he had the journal all along. So how long had they been reading his journal?
  4. What Julia actually in the thought police? I know they make it seem like she was definitely tortured in the end and possibly had a lobotomy, but there were so many suspicious things about her:
    1. She knew so much about Winston from the beginning (e.g. his name, the perfume her mom used, the chocolate).
    2. She asked him to read The Book aloud instead of reading it with him. It’s as if she wanted him to be heard by the tv.
    3. Speaking of tv, it’s suspicious that she actually pointed out the picture frame that was hiding the telescreen. “I bet that picture’s got bugs behind it.”
    4. O’Brien claimed Julia betrayed him almost immediately. But when he talks to her at the end of the book she mentions going to Room 101. Weren’t there 3 stages involved in reprogramming and torture was the last one?
  5. Why did it seem like O’Brien could read Winston’s mind? It seems like from the very beginning Winston was receiving messages from O’Brien in his dreams. I admit I wondered several times if O’Brien ever existed or if we were witnessing some Fight Club-esque type things with two sides of Winston fighting each other.
  6. If the party members were suppose to be the middle class, why did it seem like the proles lived better lives than the party members? Winston couldn’t even afford pajamas.
  7. It seems like Winston had been watched for 7 whole years before the thought police was able to get him. Did his wife actually inform the Party about him?
  8. Why did Winston hate rats so much? Part of me wonders whether he actually had previously been in Room 101 before and this was not the first time he had been tortured and reintroduced into society. That would at least explain why he had this connection with O’Brien.
